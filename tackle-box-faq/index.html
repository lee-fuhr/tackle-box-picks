Tackle Box Questions, Answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Choosing a tackle box comes down to how you fish, where you store it, and what you carry. This FAQ covers the main types, materials, sizes, and organizational features so you can match a box to your actual fishing habits without overspending or overcomplicating your gear.

What types of tackle boxes are there?

You will run into three main styles: the classic open-top box, the satchel or backpack-style soft bag, and the hard shell utility box with removable trays. The open-top box, often made of plastic with a fold-down front, gives you quick access to trays and a big storage area underneath for larger items. It is the traditional choice for shore and bank fishing. Soft bags and backpacks are lighter and easier to carry for long walks along a river or pier. They usually have padded pockets for smaller boxes or tackle bags and external slots for pliers and rods. Hard shell utility boxes are modular; you buy trays that click into a frame, so you can reconfigure compartments for different trips. Many anglers end up using a combination, a stationary box for the garage and a small backpack for the shore.

Which material holds up best: plastic, metal, or fabric?

The vast majority of modern tackle boxes are made from high-impact polypropylene or similar plastics. They resist rust, do not dent easily, and are lightweight. A well-made plastic box from companies like Plano or Flambeau can last for years even in humid conditions. Avoid cheap, thin plastic that cracks around hinges or latches. Metal boxes, usually steel or aluminum, have a vintage feel and are very sturdy, but they can rust if knocked around in a boat or left in a damp shed. Aluminum is lighter and rust-resistant but costs more. Fabric boxes and backpacks (like those from Bass Pro Shops or Penn) are convenient for mobility, but their zippers and seams are weak points. Look for reinforced stitching and water-resistant coatings. For all-day durability in saltwater, plastic or aluminum is the safer bet.

How do I choose the right size?

Size depends on how much gear you truly need for a typical trip and where you will store the box. A small utility box or a compact satchel is plenty for a few hours of panfishing with a couple of lures, hooks, and bobbers. If you fish multiple species or carry a dozen hard baits plus soft plastics and terminal tackle, you want a medium hard box (often around 18 to 22 inches wide) with at least three or four trays. For serious anglers with dozens of crankbaits, spinnerbaits, and plastics in bags, a two-story box or an extra-large tote in the boat is common. Remember that bigger boxes are heavy when full. If you hike to a spot, a backpack or a small box you can slip into a larger pack is far more practical. Measure the storage space in your car or boat before buying, many boxes are wider than you expect.

What should I look for in tray dividers and compartments?

Adjustable dividers let you customize the space for different lure sizes. Some trays have fixed walls, which work fine if you always carry similar-sized hooks and weights, but they can force awkward stacking for larger lures. Look for trays with removable dividers that snap in place without shifting. A deep bottom well is a huge plus. It can hold reel service tools, large spools of leader material, or trays you pull out for active fishing. Clear lids or see-through trays help you spot lures at a glance. Water-resistant gaskets are worth the extra cost if you fish from a kayak or in rainy conditions, they keep moisture out and prevent rust.

What are the best ways to organize tackle inside a box?

Group lures by type and size. Keep hard baits like crankbaits and jerkbaits in one tray with dividers so hooks do not tangle. Soft plastics are best stored in their original bags or in resealable pouches inside the box, they can melt or warp if pressed against hard plastic dividers for months. Terminal tackle (hooks, weights, swivels) should go in small compartment boxes or the removable trays. Use a color-coding system or labels if you fish many locations. Some anglers dedicate separate trays for finesse gear, reaction baits, and topwater. A common mistake is overstuffing. Leave room in each compartment so you can grab a lure without fighting its neighbors. Check your box after every few trips and remove any rusted hooks or torn bags to keep the rest of your gear clean.

How do I maintain a tackle box to make it last?

Rinse plastic and metal boxes with fresh water after saltwater trips, and let them dry completely before closing the lid. Salt crystals can pit hardware and corrode hooks stored inside. Soft bags and backpacks should be wiped down and hung open to air out; mold can grow in zippered pockets if they stay damp. Replace any tray that cracks or loses a divider, loose dividers let lures slide around and get damaged. Lubricate hinge and latch points occasionally with a silicone spray. For fabric boxes, treat the exterior with a water-repellent spray every season. If you store a box in a garage or shed, keep it off the concrete floor to avoid moisture wicking up through the plastic. A small desiccant pack inside the box helps in humid climates.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a tackle box for non-fishing storage?

Absolutely. Tackle boxes with adjustable dividers are excellent for storing small hardware, craft supplies, bead kits, or even socket sets. The waterproof gaskets on some models make them great for emergency kits or tool rolls on boats and RVs.

What is the difference between a tackle box and a tackle bag?

A tackle box is a rigid container that gives you stacked trays and open storage; it sits in place and offers good protection. A tackle bag is a soft-sided carryall with zippered compartments that is easier to sling over a shoulder or pack into a kayak hatch. Many anglers use both: a bag for the bank and a box for the boat or truck.

How many trays does a good tackle box need?

It depends on the range of lures you carry. For basic freshwater fishing, two to three trays (one for hard baits, one for soft plastics, one for terminal gear) are enough. More serious anglers may need four to six trays, especially if they fish multiple species or keep separate saltwater and freshwater setups.

Are water-resistant tackle boxes worth the extra cost?

For kayak anglers, wade fishermen, or anyone who fishes in rainy climates, yes. A gasketed box keeps moisture out and prevents rust on hooks and reels. For boat anglers who keep boxes under cover, a water-resistant box is less critical, but it still protects against splashes and damp rod lockers.

Should I buy a tackle box with a removable top tray?

A removable top tray adds flexibility. You can lift it out to reach the bottom well or snap it onto a boat seat for quick access. It also makes cleaning the box easier. Most quality medium-sized boxes include this feature.

What is the best way to pack a tackle box for a long trip?

Pack heavy items like large hard baits and bulky pliers in the bottom well, and lighter items in the top trays. Use separate small bags or pouches for hooks and weights to prevent tangling. Leave some empty space so trays can slide in and out without jamming. A checklist helps you avoid forgetting key lures for the species you will target.

In shortNo single box works for every trip. Think about your most common fishing style, shore, boat, kayak, or hike-in, and choose a box that balances capacity, portability, and durability. A well-organized tackle box saves time on the water and protects your investment in lures and hooks. Buy a quality box that fits your routine, and you will not need to replace it for years.
